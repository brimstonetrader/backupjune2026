To prove that RWYW is weakly asymptotically safe, we must show that \[\lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{T} \left(\sum_{t=0}^{T} v_i - u_i(a_i^t, a_{-i}^t)\right)  \geq 0\]. 

Suppose RWYW is playing a game against an optimal opponent. WLOG, assume the winning payoff is 1, and the expected game value is 0. It will be in one of two states. The cumulative payoff cannot be positive asymptotically if the opponent is optimal, it must be at most zero. It is possible for a nemesis algorithm to needsdefine{bait} RWYW into \needsdefine{squander}ing its winnings when it has a positive cumulative payoff, but such a strategy will not work when RWYW has cumulative nonpositive payoff. At that point, both players will be playing GTO, and the asymptotic behavior can be modeled as a one-dimensional random walk.

It has been shown that a 1d random walk of $n$ steps has expected distance from the origin of $O(\sqrt{n})$ (constant factor of sqrt(2/pi)). The walk here is different in that zero has transition probabilities of equally staying in place or moving to -1. This does not make a difference in the expected distance, as the absolute value is unchanged between a standard walk and this half-line walk. Thus, asymptotically, RWYW against a worst case opponent will achieve payoff of O(1/sqrt(n)) per round, which asymptotically vanishes. Thus, RWYW is weakly asymptotically safe.

Strong Asymptotic Safety is a more difficult condition to verify. Again we must prove that the expected value per-round vanishes to zero over time. 

I believe a counterexample to it being S.A.S. is as follows. Suppose a nemesis strategy plays 100 rounds by GTO, making those rounds a random walk for both players. The odds are 16% that the payoff to RWYW will be \geq 10, due to the standard deviation being ~10 and the odds being 1/2 that it will wind up on the positive side. In those 16%, suppose our nemesis is maximally exploitative, extracting -10*c in expectation. This is a constant number, asymptotically. 

-> The issue. The other 84% is on average -2.9. The assumption that 1/4 will be +10 only holds when the walk begins at zero - this is gradually not true as the exploitation strategy continues.


A counterexample would need to show a constant negative EV over time. To do this, a strategy would need to reliably follow a cycle where it's able to find a natural positive fluctuation of the random walk and exploit that. I don't believe that this is possible, because the law of the iterated logarithm states that the expected maximal extreme of a random walk is sqrt(2n log(log(n)))
